Prisoner's dilemma

A five-part series on cooperation, defection, and what happens when you repeat the game

One simple game. Five ways to look at it.

The prisoner's dilemma is one of the most studied problems in game theory, economics, and evolutionary biology. Two players each choose to cooperate or defect. Defecting is always better for you individually — but if both defect, both lose. This series builds from the basic game up through signal noise, tournaments, and spatial evolution to show how cooperation can emerge even when it isn't the individually rational choice.

Series

  1. Part 1 — Repeated prisoner's dilemma — pit two strategies against each other and watch who wins
  2. Part 2 — Signal noise — design your own strategy and see how miscommunication disrupts cooperation
  3. Part 3 — Round-robin tournament — every strategy plays every other under noise and random game length
  4. Part 4 — Evolutionary simulation — score determines survival; watch which strategies take over the population
  5. Part 5 — Spatial evolution — agents on a grid, interacting only with neighbors; cooperation can finally cluster and survive

Parts 4 and 5 together answer the question: why does tit-for-tat dominate in Axelrod's famous tournament but lose in a random-pairing world? The answer is geography.
