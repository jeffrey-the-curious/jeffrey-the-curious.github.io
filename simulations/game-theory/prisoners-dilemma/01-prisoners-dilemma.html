Repeated prisoner's dilemma

Explore how cooperation emerges (or doesn't) when two players meet repeatedly. This is a foundation for many concepts in industrial economics and antitrust.

Home Simulations Game Theory

Two players. Each round: cooperate or defect. What strategy wins?

In a single game, defection is always the strictly dominant strategy. Why then do we see so much cooperation in life (social animals like humans) and business? Pick strategies for both players and run the simulation to see who accumulates more points, and why.

Strategy glossary

Tit-for-tat
Cooperate on round 1, then copy whatever the opponent did last round. This rewards cooperation, punishes defection immediately.
Always cooperate
Never defects. Achieves mutual benefit against cooperative opponents but is easily exploited.
Always defect
Never cooperates. This is the dominant finite option. It cannot be "beaten", but also cannot reach any mutual cooperation that clearly does well in the real world.
Grim trigger
Cooperate until the opponent defects once, then defect forever. This is maximally punishing, and is inflexible regarding forgiveness, random events, or signal error.
Tit-for-two-tats
Only retaliates after two consecutive defections. More forgiving than tit-for-tat which handles random events or signal errors better. This strategy is less easily baited into unfortunate cycles of punishment.
Random (50/50)
Cooperates or defects with equal probability each round. This has no memory or feelings.
